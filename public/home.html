Protein

Protein is essential to good health. The very origin of the word — from the Greek protos, meaning "first" — reflects protein's top-shelf status in human nutrition. You need it to put meat on your bones and to make hair, blood, connective tissue, antibodies, enzymes, and more. It's common for athletes and bodybuilders to wolf down extra protein to bulk up. But the message the rest of us often get is that our daily protein intake is too high.

Kwashiorkor

Kwashiorkor is a type of malnutrition characterized by severe protein deficiency. It causes fluid retention and a swollen, distended abdomen. Kwashiorkor most commonly affects children, particularly in developing countries with high levels of poverty and food insecurity. People with kwashiorkor may have food to eat, but not enough protein.

How much protein do you need?

  • Protein should account for 10% to 35% of your calories. So, if your daily needs are 2,000 calories, that's 200-700 calories from protein or 50-175 grams.
  • The recommended dietary allowance to prevent deficiency for an average sedentary adult is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight.
  • For those over age 40-50, protein needs increase to about 1-1.2 grams per kilogram.
  • People who exercise regularly or lift weights need about 1.1-1.7 grams per kilogram.

Daily protein intake for women

For a 50-year-old woman who weighs 140 pounds and is sedentary (doesn't exercise), that translates into 53 grams of protein a day. During pregnancy, protein needs of women go up. Experts recommend 75 to 100 grams of protein a day for the developing fetal tissue, as well as the enlarging placenta, breasts, and blood supply. You should check with your doctor on your specific protein needs.

Does protein have any negative health effects?

Some research suggests casual links between high protein diets and kidney function impairments. However, there is no evidence to suggest a direct cause and effect between the two. Further research has shown that following a high-protein diet can aggravate preexisting chronic kidney disease. People with preexisting kidney conditions should consult their doctor before drastically changing their protein intake.

Overall, there’s no evidence that a reasonably high protein intake has any adverse effects in healthy people trying to optimize their health.

How to get enough protein in your diet

There is a broad range of foods that are high in protein. These include:

  • Tofu, tempeh, and other meat substitutes
  • Lean meats
  • Fish
  • Eggs
  • Milk
  • Greek yogurt
  • Quinoa
  • Legumes
  • Nuts

Most people generally don’t need to track their protein intake. If you’re healthy and trying to stay that way, simply eating quality protein sources with most of your meals, along with nutritious plant foods, should bring your intake to an optimal range.

Fats

According to current dietary guidelines, a healthy adult should consume between 20-35% of their daily calories from fat, which translates to roughly 0.5-1 gram of fat per kilogram of body weight depending on their activity level and overall calorie needs.

Does your body need fat?

Fat is an important source of energy you get from the food you eat. Your body needs fat to grow cells, protect your organs, and absorb fat-soluble vitamins...

Types of Fat

Saturated Fats

Saturated fats are found in animal products like meat and full-fat dairy. They also occur in tropical oils like palm and coconut...

  • Meats like beef, lamb, and pork
  • Processed meats like bacon and sausage
  • Chicken skin
  • Full-fat dairy products like cream, cheese, and butter
  • Tropical oils, like coconut and palm

Unsaturated fats are typically liquid at room temperature. They come from plant sources and fatty fish, like salmon...

  • Nontropical vegetable oils, like olive, canola, and soybean
  • Fatty fish, like bluefin tuna, salmon, and sardines
  • Nuts and seeds like walnuts, flaxseeds, and sunflower seeds
  • Other foods like avocados and peanut butter

Health Risks of Excess Fat Intake

  • Weight Gain: Fat is high in calories, with 9 calories per gram, which is more than twice the amount found in carbohydrates and protein.
  • Heart Disease and Stroke: Eating too much saturated fat can raise your LDL (bad) cholesterol.
  • Cancer: Some studies suggest that high-fat diets may be linked to several cancers.
  • Digestive Distress: Too much fat can cause bloating, gas, and loose stools.

Carbohydrates

Moderately active individuals should aim for 5-7 grams of carbohydrates per kilogram of body weight.

Daily Calorie Intake

The Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommend that carbohydrates make up 45–65% of your total daily calories. For example, if you eat 2,000 calories per day, you should consume 225–325 grams of carbohydrates.

Weight Loss

If you're trying to lose weight, you can try eating 100–150 grams of carbohydrates per day. You can also try breaking up your carbohydrates evenly throughout the day, with 40–50 grams per meal.

Understanding Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are a type of macronutrient found in many foods and beverages. Most carbs occur naturally in plant-based foods, such as grains. Food manufacturers also add carbs to processed foods in the form of starch or added sugar.

  • Fruits
  • Vegetables
  • Milk
  • Nuts
  • Grains
  • Seeds
  • Beans, peas, and lentils

Types of Carbohydrates

  • Sugar: The simplest form of carbohydrate, naturally found in fruits, vegetables, and milk.
  • Starch: A complex carbohydrate found in vegetables, grains, and beans.
  • Fiber: Essential for digestive health, found in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.

Carbohydrates and Your Health

Carbohydrates are vital for energy, disease prevention, and weight control. They are the body's main fuel source, breaking down into simple sugars during digestion and providing glucose for energy.

Protecting Against Disease

Whole grains and dietary fiber help lower the risk of heart disease, stroke, obesity, and certain cancers. Fiber is also essential for optimal digestive health.

Controlling Weight

Eating plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains can help control weight by promoting fullness with fewer calories.

Choose Your Carbohydrates Wisely

  • Focus on fiber-rich fruits and vegetables.
  • Choose whole grains over refined grains.
  • Stick to low-fat dairy products.
  • Eat more beans, peas, and lentils.
  • Limit added sugars.
